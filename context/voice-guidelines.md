# Wine Spies Voice & Tone Guidelines

## The Brand in One Sentence

Wine Spies finds killer wines at insane prices and tells real people about them in plain English — with a wink.

---

## Core Voice Pillars

### 1. Direct
Get to the point. The deal is the headline. Don't warm up.
> ❌ "In the rolling hills of Tuscany, one estate has been quietly crafting..."  
> ✅ "96 points. 45% off. Two barrels made it to the US. That's it."

### 2. Conversational
Write like you're texting a wine-obsessed friend who trusts you. Not like a newsletter. Not like a wine review.
> ❌ "The nose presents blackberry macerated in cloves..."  
> ✅ "Dark fruit, cocoa, a little spice — basically everything you want in a Cab."

### 3. Energetic
There's real excitement here. Big discount. Great wine. Limited quantity. Let that energy come through without screaming.
> ✅ "Was $110. Now $55. Nothing at this price even comes close."

### 4. Witty (but not trying too hard)
A well-placed analogy or pop culture reference can do more than three paragraphs of wine description.
> ✅ "The Corvette C8 of Chardonnay — runs with the big boys and costs a fraction."
> ✅ "Moving fast like a dachshund race."
> ✅ "Faster than a fresh drop of toilet paper at Costco."

### 5. Credible
Back big claims with real proof: point scores, vintage rep, winemaker pedigree, neighbor vineyards.
> ✅ "Grown right next to Sassicaia — which sells for 5x more."

## Reading Level Target

**Flesch-Kincaid Grade 5–7.** Always. No exceptions.

Test your copy. If it scores above 8, simplify.

**Tools:**
- Hemingway App (hemingwayapp.com)
- Readable.com

## Vocabulary: Use / Avoid

**Use freely:**
- Operatives (our customers)
- Score (point score)
- Vintage
- Estate
- Cult / library / single-vineyard
- Napa, Sonoma, Rutherford, Russian River, etc.
- FOMO, limited, almost gone, act fast, moving fast

**Avoid:**
- Delicious
- Elegant
- Sophisticated  
- Complex (overused — show the complexity instead)
- Journey (never)
- Curated (never)
- Delight / delightful

---

## The Spy Theme

Customers are called **"Operatives."** That's the main spy touchpoint in daily copy.

The theme lives in the brand's visual identity and email design more than in the copy itself. Use "Operatives" naturally. Don't force spy metaphors into every piece.

**Acceptable:** "Heads up, Operatives — this one won't last the day."  
**Too much:** "Mission: acquire this bottle before enemy forces deplete inventory."

---

## Scarcity Language — Make It Real

Scarcity at Wine Spies is genuine. Lean into it. Be specific when you can.
- "Only 20 barrels exist. Two made it to the US. We have barely enough."
- "Probably won't last the day."
- "Arriving directly from the library — once it's gone, it's gone."
- "We bought every case we could. Was it enough? Probably not."

---

## The Lead (First 1–2 Sentences)

The lead's only job is to make them keep reading. Options that work:
- The deal up front: points + discount + "yes please"
- A bold comparison: "Runs with [cult wine] at a fraction of the price"
- A punchy question or statement that creates curiosity
- A pop culture or lifestyle analogy

Never start with: the region, the winery's history, or a tasting note.
