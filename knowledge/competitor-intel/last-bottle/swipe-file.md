## HOW LAST BOTTLE WRITES: MASTER VOICE SUMMARY

Last Bottle's voice is a single, hyperkinetic narrator who writes like a sports announcer calling the greatest wine deal in human history. Every email is a performance. The register is: breathless enthusiasm + genuine expertise + zero pretension. They never sound like a wine writer. They sound like your most knowledgeable friend who also happens to be extremely caffeinated.

**Core voice mechanics:**
- ALL CAPS for emphasis (used liberally, never ironically)
- Exclamation points as punctuation, not exception
- Rhetorical questions followed by immediate self-answers
- Personal asides and tangents that build intimacy
- Score + price as the two anchors everything else orbits
- Flavor copy written as sensation, not description
- Every email ends with a scarcity trigger

**Reading level target:** FK Grade 5–7 (same as Wine Spies)  
**Tone distance from Wine Spies:** LBW is louder, more personal, more self-indulgent. Wine Spies is sharper, more coded, more brand-contained.

## TECHNIQUE 1: PRICE ANCHORING

The single most repeated structural move in LBW copy. Price is never stated once — it's repeated, italicized, compared to retail, compared to competitors, and compared to itself. The deal IS the story.

---

### SWIPE 1-A: The Repeat-and-Escalate
> "Under $40 for an Estate of the Gods wine like this? Ah, TUSCANY, glorious Tuscany shines bright again!"

**Why it works:** The rhetorical question recaps the price, then the emotional release ("Ah, TUSCANY") validates the purchase emotionally. The reader feels like they *discovered* the deal, not that they were sold to. The wine name embedded as a superlative ("Estate of the Gods") makes the price feel even more absurd.

**Remix prompt:**
> Take this price anchor structure — rhetorical question + price + emotional release — and rewrite it for [WINE NAME] at [PRICE POINT]. The emotional release should reference the wine's region or producer prestige. Keep it under 2 sentences.

---

### SWIPE 1-B: The Savings Math Drop
> "I had my mathematically inclined colleague do the math…we're talking about $115 in savings, good people!"

**Why it works:** The self-deprecating attribution ("my mathematically inclined colleague") is disarming and funny. It makes the savings feel independently verified without sounding like a press release. The comma-separated "good people" is a classic LBW intimacy marker.

**Remix prompt:**
> Rewrite a savings callout for [WINE] at [PRICE] (originally [RETAIL]). Include a winking attribution of the math to someone or something other than yourself. Keep the tone casual and warm, under 2 sentences.

---

### SWIPE 1-C: The Stacked Discount Structure
> "UNDER $30!! Better still — you can add on an additional 25% OFF with the promo code below!"

**Why it works:** Two-stage reveal. The first price lands. Then "Better still" signals a second gift. The double exclamation after the first price creates a breath of disbelief before the escalation. Pure deal momentum.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a two-stage price reveal for [WINE] at [PRICE]. First land the price with disbelief. Then introduce a secondary incentive (promo code, bundle, free shipping). Use "Better still" or a structural equivalent as the hinge.

## TECHNIQUE 2: URGENCY AND SCARCITY

LBW treats scarcity as both a closer and an atmosphere. It's baked into CTAs but also woven into body copy as ambient pressure.

---

### SWIPE 2-A: The Ambient Scarcity Riff
> "Only 935 cases made…EVER — and we pulled a rabbit to snag as much as humanly possible to share with y'all. Barely enough…even for a slow, pre-St. Paddy's Sunday — so do yourself a solid, take the requisite 60 seconds it takes to fly to your cart, and LOAD UP!"

**Why it works:** Production figure → effort signal ("pulled a rabbit") → scarcity made personal ("barely enough…even for") → specific time frame → micro-CTA with a time estimate that minimizes friction. The "60 seconds" is a masterstroke — it makes acting feel effortless. "LOAD UP" is pure velocity.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a scarcity close for [WINE] with [CASE COUNT] made. Include: a production number, an effort signal showing how hard it was to get, a personalized scarcity moment, a friction-minimizing CTA. Max 3 sentences.

---

### SWIPE 2-B: The Signature Close
> "While it lasts!"
> "While it lasts, friends!!"

**Why it works:** It's so simple it's almost aggressive. The repetition across every email trains the reader to associate these three words with urgency and action. It functions like a Pavlovian trigger by email 10. The addition of "friends" softens it just enough to not feel like a threat.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write 3 variations of a closing urgency line under 6 words. One should include an address to the reader (operative, agent, member, etc.). One should be almost purely tonal. One should embed a product reference.

---

### SWIPE 2-C: The Event Scarcity Frame (Marathon)
> "At any point during the event, there will be only ONE wine on the site, and each offer will only be available for an extremely limited time (sometimes only SECONDS). You've gotta be quick, or you'll miss it."

**Why it works:** Scarcity made mechanical, not emotional. By explaining the *system*, LBW turns FOMO into a logical inevitability. "Sometimes only SECONDS" is the spike. The rhyming couplet quality of "quick, or you'll miss it" makes it memorable and sticky.

**Remix prompt:**
> Explain a limited-time or limited-quantity mechanic for [EVENT/OFFER] in a way that makes scarcity feel structural, not manipulative. Use a parenthetical to land the most extreme case. End with a simple if/then statement under 8 words.

## TECHNIQUE 3: FLAVOR AND SENSORY COPY

This is where LBW earns its reputation. Their tasting notes are written as bodily experiences, not wine vocabulary exercises. The goal is to make the reader *taste* the wine before they buy it.

---

### SWIPE 3-A: The Sensation Avalanche
> "The explosive sensations of summer strawberries, and white cherries, with flinty, river rock intensity, and the zippy, zesty sensation of freshly peeled oranges, and lingering, beguiling aromas of star jasmine, and fresh-cut grass, with this watermelon agua fresca finish…YES!!!"

**Why it works:** The comma-and structure creates an additive, breathless momentum — you can't pause to evaluate, you just keep tasting. Each element is concrete and familiar (no "hints of minerality"). "YES!!!" at the end is the reader's release valve — the writer says what they're feeling so the reader doesn't have to.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a sensation avalanche tasting note for [WINE] using the comma-and structure. 5–8 elements. Each element should be a concrete image or sensation (no abstract wine terms). Close with a one-word or one-syllable emotional release.

---

### SWIPE 3-B: The Physical Metaphor Close
> "This baby is pumping like 70's-era Schwarzenegger, and finishing stronger than a 'last call' shot of Fernet."

**Why it works:** Two pop culture physical comparisons that translate abstract structure into something the reader can visualize and feel. "Pumping" applies a body-building metaphor to wine texture. The Fernet close is inside baseball for wine people — it creates an in-group recognition moment. Both are funny without undercutting the wine's quality.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a 2-part physical metaphor close for [WINE]'s structure and finish. First metaphor: something from pop culture, sports, or physical performance that conveys texture or body. Second metaphor: something that conveys finish length or intensity. Keep it under 2 sentences, surprising, and brand-specific.

---

### SWIPE 3-C: The Texture-First Note
> "It's inky, and unbelievably rich, with MOUNTAINS of structure, and this juicy, decadent core of black and red bramble berries, then roasted plum tarts, and cherries jubilee, and heady kirsch, black pepper, strawberry compote, and a dusting of mocha powder."

**Why it works:** Leads with mouthfeel (inky, rich) before fruit — this is the opposite of how most tasting notes are written, and it immediately tells you what to *expect* before you taste. "MOUNTAINS of structure" is a perfect all-caps moment because structure is abstract; the capitals make it concrete and massive. The list is indulgent and long in a way that reads as generosity, not padding.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a texture-first tasting note for [WINE]. Open with 2 mouthfeel descriptors. Escalate to one all-caps structural claim. Follow with a fruit/flavor list of at least 5 elements that builds in richness from first to last. No wine jargon.

## TECHNIQUE 4: CREDENTIAL AND AUTHORITY DROPS

LBW uses winemaker and critic credentialing as a form of social proof, but always told as a *story* rather than a list. The credentials exist to raise the perceived improbability of the price.

---

### SWIPE 4-A: The Résumé Riff
> "He's already had his hands in nearly a dozen 100-point wines, working alongside Russell Bevan first, and now Jesse Katz. He's also gone global — working for New Zealand's cultiest of cult producers, Craggy Range, as well as another 100 point icon of global winemaking…Paul Hobbs at his premier winery, Viña Cobos."

**Why it works:** Credentials delivered as a career story rather than a list. The em-dash acts as a storytelling hinge. Name-dropping is specific enough to signal real expertise to wine people, but framed accessibly enough not to alienate newcomers. "Cultiest of cult producers" is self-aware and funny.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a winemaker credential riff for [WINEMAKER] at [PRODUCER]. Include at least 2 previous notable connections. Deliver as a mini career arc, not a bio. One element should include a superlative that's slightly self-aware. Keep under 4 sentences.

---

### SWIPE 4-B: The Score-as-Bombshell
> "Rosé almost NEVER pulls down scores like that — but this is RED CAR!"

**Why it works:** The context before the score ("almost NEVER") makes the score feel even more extraordinary. The em-dash pivot to the producer name creates a reveal moment. The reader feels like they're in on something rare, not just being told a fact.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a score reveal for [WINE] at [SCORE POINTS] that includes: a context statement explaining why that score is unusual for this category or producer, an em-dash pivot, and the producer name as the punch. Under 2 sentences.

---

### SWIPE 4-C: The Critic Quote Setup
> "when the critics croon on that 'this bold, plush, juicy and inviting flagship red is the freshest and most electric to date' you better listen!"

**Why it works:** "Croon" is a perfect verb — it dignifies the critic while also making their enthusiasm feel human and felt. The embedded quote is short enough to land before attribution fatigue sets in. "You better listen!" turns a third-party opinion into a direct command addressed to the reader.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a critic quote setup for [WINE] with this structure: verb describing how the critic expressed the opinion (not "said" or "noted") + short embedded quote fragment + direct command to the reader. Keep the command informal and under 5 words.

## TECHNIQUE 5: LEAD HOOKS AND EMAIL OPENS

LBW never opens with the wine. They open with energy, event, or emotion, and let the wine arrive mid-paragraph like a character entering a scene.

---

### SWIPE 5-A: The Calendar/Holiday Hook
> "Break out the shamrocks! Banish the snakes from the land! St. Paddy's Day is here, and we're kicking off our day of jigs with this KILLER Alexander Valley Cabernet Sauvignon for UNDER $30!!"

**Why it works:** Two imperative sentences create immediate kinetic energy. The holiday frame is familiar but the payoff — a Cabernet, not a shamrock-shaped cookie — creates comedic contrast. Price is in the same sentence as the hook, so by the time you've engaged emotionally, the value has already landed.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a holiday/calendar lead hook for [OCCASION] for [WINE] at [PRICE]. Open with 1–2 short imperative sentences that evoke the occasion. Transition into the wine offer in the same sentence as the price. Keep the juxtaposition between the occasion and the wine slightly surprising.

---

### SWIPE 5-B: The Personal Tangent Hook
> "It's my BIRTHDAY…and while I am still recovering slowly from yesterday's over-indulgence with many-a-pint-o'the-black-stuff at my favorite Irish pub…I can crawl towards the rainbow's end, and the proverbial pot o'gold that awaits with a glass of this!"

**Why it works:** Opens with a personal confession that immediately humanizes the narrator. The recovery imagery creates sympathy and humor. "Crawl towards the rainbow's end" is poetic in a way that earns the metaphor without feeling precious. The wine arrives as a literal cure — perfect desire architecture.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a personal tangent hook for [WINE] where the narrator arrives at the wine through a personal circumstance (a celebration, recovery, routine, mood). The wine should function as the solution or destination, not the starting point. Keep the tangent to 2 sentences before pivoting.

---

### SWIPE 5-C: The Score-Leads Everything Hook
> "94 POINTS! Spring is here — and summer isn't far behind...and trust me when I say you are gonna want some of this EPIC Sonoma Coast Rosé!"

**Why it works:** Score as first word, no preamble. Then a seasonal frame that creates forward momentum (not "it's spring" but "summer isn't far behind"). "Trust me when I say" is the narrator inserting themselves as guarantor — it shifts the relationship from brand-to-customer to friend-to-friend.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a score-first hook for [WINE] at [SCORE POINTS] in [REGION/CATEGORY]. Open with the score. Follow with a seasonal or situational frame that creates anticipation. Close the opening line with a trust signal — something that positions the narrator as a friend vouching for this, not a retailer selling it.

## TECHNIQUE 6: PRODUCER STORYTELLING

LBW uses producer stories as a credibility vector but also as entertainment — the story has to earn its length by being interesting, not just informative.

---

### SWIPE 6-A: The Personality-Led Bio
> "Roberto Cavalli, who brought sand-blasted jeans and exotic animal prints to the forefront of 80's fashion (the shame, the shame!), ran this estate for years with his son, Tomasso."

**Why it works:** The parenthetical "(the shame, the shame!)" is pure LBW — it acknowledges a shared cultural memory with a wink, making the reader feel like they're in on a joke with the narrator. It also makes Roberto Cavalli a *character*, not a credential. The story becomes memorable precisely because of the fashion tangent.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a 2-sentence producer bio for [PRODUCER] that includes one surprising, non-wine fact about their background or identity. Include a parenthetical that acknowledges the absurdity or delight of that fact. The wine or estate should arrive in the second sentence.

---

### SWIPE 6-B: The Mythology Build
> "Champagne Piollot has been around since the 19th century, but it wasn't until the current generation...that it began maintaining a more acute focus and connection to the land."

**Why it works:** Classic hero's journey structure compressed into a single sentence. Long heritage → recent transformation → implied wine quality as result. The phrase "connection to the land" does a lot of work without being cliché because it's immediately followed by specifics.

**Remix prompt:**
> Write a mythology build for [PRODUCER] using this structure: founding era or heritage claim + turning point marker ("but it wasn't until...") + transformation agent (person, philosophy, event) + implied quality result. Keep to 2 sentences.

## TECHNIQUE 7: SUBJECT LINES

LBW subject lines are direct, score-anchored, and often use punctuation as a voice device. They never tease — they reveal.

---

### SWIPE 7-A: Score + Category Frame
> "96 Point Italian Cabernet Blend!"  
> "95 Point Sonoma Cab!"  
> "97 Points! Transcendent $60 Blanc de Blancs!"

**Why it works:** Score first, category second, price optional. No mystery, no clickbait — they're selling to a list that wants to know immediately if this is for them. The exclamation point is not enthusiasm; it's a voice signature.

---

### SWIPE 7-B: Price + Outcome Frame
> "Killer Cali-Rosé, Unbeatable Price!"  
> "Crazy Price On Cali-Cab!"  
> "Elegant Bouzy Grand Cru Champagne, Only $49!"

**Why it works:** Adjective-Wine-Price structure. The adjective is always a quality signal, the price is always the payoff. "Only $49" works because Champagne buyers know Bouzy Grand Cru is a $75+ category — the price anchors relative to implicit expectation.

---

### SWIPE 7-C: The Event/Announcement Subject
> "Spring MARATHON Arrives Next Week! 🍷"

**Why it works:** ALL CAPS for the key noun, emoji for visual distinction in inbox, temporal specificity ("Next Week") for urgency. Emoji use is restrained — one, functional, not decorative.

**Remix prompt for all subject lines:**
> Write 3 subject line variations for [WINE] at [PRICE] with [SCORE POINTS]. Variation 1: Score-first. Variation 2: Price-first. Variation 3: A quality descriptor + price combination. Each under 8 words. Use exclamation points as voice, not for decoration.

## SCORING/CRITIQUE RUBRIC — HOW TO EVALUATE COPY AGAINST LBW STANDARD

Use this rubric to score Wine Spies or Archival copy against the LBW benchmark, or to critique drafts.

| Element | LBW Does This | Score 1–5 |
|---|---|---|
| Price arrives within first 3 sentences | Almost always | — |
| Flavor copy uses concrete/physical language (no abstract wine terms) | Consistently | — |
| At least one credential or score is contextualized (not just stated) | Always | — |
| CTA contains a friction-minimizing element | Usually | — |
| Producer story contains one non-wine surprise | Often | — |
| Subject line leads with score or price | Majority | — |
| Narrator inserts personal voice at least once | Every email | — |
| Scarcity signal appears in body AND close | Every email | — |

## VOICE CONTRAST: LBW vs. WINE SPIES

Use this when you need to *not* sound like LBW — or to calibrate how far Wine Spies should go in a given direction.

| Dimension | Last Bottle | Wine Spies |
|---|---|---|
| Narrator identity | Personal, named, confessional | Brand voice (the Spy), character-contained |
| Energy register | 11/10, sustained | 8–9/10, controlled bursts |
| Humor style | Self-deprecating, pop culture | Wry, coded, brand-specific |
| Price framing | Explicit, repeated, celebrated | Implied via mission/deal-finding |
| Credential use | Storytelling, tangential | Tight, authoritative |
| Scarcity language | Ambient throughout | Specific, tactical |
| Tasting notes | Sensation avalanche, long | Punchy, shorter, FK 5–7 |
| Closes | "While it lasts!" | "Go operative / Your mission..." |
